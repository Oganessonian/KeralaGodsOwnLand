Kerala - Wildlife & Biodiversity

Kerala's Western Ghats form one of the world's eight hottest biodiversity hotspots. This mountain range shelters unique species found nowhere else on Earth, including the endangered Lion-tailed Macaque and Nilgiri Tahr. Beyond mammals, the region boasts exceptional botanical diversity, highlighted by the Neelakurinji flower that transforms entire hillsides blue every twelve years. Protected by numerous wildlife sanctuaries and national parks, these ecosystems range from high-altitude grasslands to tropical rainforests, creating vital corridors for endangered species and making Kerala a significant conservation zone within India.

1. Nilgiri Tahr

The Nilgiri Tahr (Nilgiritragus hylocrius) is an endangered mountain ungulate endemic to the Western Ghats. This sturdy wild goat, distinguished by its curved horns and coarse fur, thrives on the steep, rocky grasslands of high altitudes. Eravikulam National Park in Kerala hosts its largest global population, offering critical protection. Unlike most ungulates, Tahrs are skilled climbers, navigating near-vertical cliffs with remarkable agility. Conservation efforts have gradually increased their numbers, but habitat fragmentation continues to threaten this iconic species, symbolizing the fragile balance of Kerala's high-altitude ecosystems.

2. Lion-Tailed Macaque

The Lion-Tailed Macaque (Macaca silenus) is one of the world's most endangered primates, endemic to the Western Ghats. Identified by its striking silver-white mane surrounding a black face, this Old-World monkey is predominantly arboreal, rarely descending to the forest floor. It thrives in the dense, evergreen forests of Silent Valley National Park and Parambikulam Tiger Reserve. Unlike other macaques, it is largely frugivorous and possesses a notably shy and quiet demeanor. Habitat loss from deforestation and plantations poses the gravest threat to its survival, making its conservation a critical priority.

3. Neelakurinji

Strobilanthes kunthiana, commonly known as Neelakurinji, is a unique shrub endemic to the Western Ghats, famous for its spectacular mass flowering that occurs only once every twelve years. When it blooms, the hillsides of Munnar and surrounding areas are transformed into a breathtaking sea of blue and purple flowersâ€”a phenomenon that last occurred in 2018. This botanical marvel plays a crucial role in the local ecosystem, influencing the life cycles of pollinators and other species. The next anticipated bloom is in 2030, making it a rare natural event that draws botanists and tourists from around the world.
